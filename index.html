Chapter 1 Live FRED Data

The Evidence: Trimmed Mean vs. Headline vs. Core

Cleveland Fed 16% trimmed-mean CPI strips the highest and lowest 8% of components — extracting trend inflation without arbitrary exclusions.

💡

Why Trimmed Mean Wins

The 16% trimmed mean symmetrically drops outliers (used cars, energy, pandemic distortions) without the fixed exclusions of "core." Empirical work from the Dallas and Cleveland Feds shows it has the strongest signal-to-noise ratio for underlying inflation pressure.

Chapter 1b Computed

Headline–Trimmed Mean Spread

When headline exceeds trimmed mean, transitory shocks (energy, food) are inflating the number. A positive spread = more room to cut.

Chapter 2

Neo-Wicksellian Doctrine vs. Reactionary Function

Robertson argues post-GFC monetary policy became an expectations-management game.

Post-GFC Invention

"Inflation Expectations"

Pre-GFC, expectations played a role but were less dominant. At the zero lower bound, the Fed turned expectations into the primary transmission channel.

Surveys (Michigan) and TIPS breakevens do influence wage/price setting. Dismissing them entirely ignores forward-looking behavior — but their post-GFC elevation to primary policy lever is Robertson's target.

Academic Construct

Neo-Wicksellian Models

Modern DSGE frameworks (Woodford et al.) distinguish the "natural" real rate from the market rate. The Fed sets policy to close the gap.

These models struggled with post-GFC lowflation and the 2021–2022 surge. Robertson calls it a "device" — a constructed lens where models dominate over data.

Warsh Proposal

Reactionary Function

Drop forward guidance. Each FOMC meeting is a fresh proposition based purely on contemporaneous data. No dot plots telegraphing the future.

This reduces credibility risk when conditions change. But markets may overreact. The trade-off: clarity of rules vs. flexibility of discretion.

Chapter 5

Steel-Man / Straw-Man

Robertson's view is credible and chart-backed — but no framework is without trade-offs.

✓ Strengths

  • Trimmed mean is empirically superior in noisy times
  • Clear reaction function improves market predictability
  • Reduces credibility risk from forward guidance
  • Aligns with pre-GFC "rules vs. discretion" literature
  • Timely: trimmed mean justifies path to neutral rate

✗ Limitations

  • Expectations aren't purely "invented" — they matter empirically
  • Taylor rules failed real-time tests (prescribed tightness in 2008–09)
  • Output gaps are notoriously hard to measure in real time
  • Warsh operates in a politicized environment
  • Quick easing risks re-igniting inflation if supply shocks return
Conclusion

Bottom Line

Robertson's thread is a concise, chart-backed plea for humility in monetary policy: use the cleanest inflation signal, react to data now rather than promise the future, and let rules guide rather than models dominate.
